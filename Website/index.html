Aim of the study ▼

Traditional pairwise meta-analyses are limited to summarizing direct evidence from randomized controlled trials that compare two interventions. When many competing treatments exist and head-to-head trials are scarce, this approach falls short.

Network meta-analysis (NMA) extends conventional meta-analysis by simultaneously synthesizing both direct and indirect evidence across a network of interventions, allowing relative effects to be estimated even between treatments that have never been tested head-to-head, and providing a framework for ranking therapies by efficacy and tolerability.

Triple-Negative Breast Cancer (TNBC)

TNBC accounts for roughly 10–20% of all breast cancers and is an aggressive subtype with a high propensity for early relapse and poor prognosis. Because hormone receptors and HER2 are absent, endocrine therapy and HER2-directed agents are not options, leaving cytotoxic chemotherapy as the mainstay of treatment.

For early-stage disease, neoadjuvant chemotherapy is standard. Achieving a pathologic complete response (pCR) after neoadjuvant therapy is strongly associated with better long-term outcomes — but with conventional anthracycline–taxane regimens, only about 35–45% of patients reach pCR, leaving substantial room to improve.

Importance

Despite improvements in pathologic complete response (pCR) achieved by novel therapeutic combinations, the optimal neoadjuvant regimen for triple-negative breast cancer remains unclear — particularly when efficacy must be weighed against treatment-related hematologic toxicity.

Objective

To address this clinical gap, we conducted a systematic review and network meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials to evaluate the comparative efficacy and safety of neoadjuvant treatments in early-stage TNBC, with pCR as the primary outcome of interest.

Highlights

Phase 2-3 randomized controlled trials of neoadjuvant therapy in early and locally advanced TNBC were synthesized from Medline, Embase, and the Cochrane Library through August 2025, covering standard chemotherapy, PD-1 / PD-L1 inhibitors, and PARP inhibitors.
