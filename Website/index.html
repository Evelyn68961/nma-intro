Traditional pairwise meta-analyses are limited to summarizing direct evidence from randomized controlled trials that compare two interventions. When many competing treatments exist and head-to-head trials are scarce, this approach falls short.

Network meta-analysis (NMA) extends conventional meta-analysis by simultaneously synthesizing both direct and indirect evidence across a network of interventions, allowing relative effects to be estimated even between treatments that have never been tested head-to-head, and providing a framework for ranking therapies by efficacy and tolerability.

Direct comparison (head-to-head trial exists) Indirect comparison (inferred via the network)

In TNBC, most novel agents have been tested against the chemotherapy backbone (ChT) but rarely against each other. NMA leverages this shared comparator to estimate, for example, PD-1 vs PARPi even though no trial directly compares them.

Two key assumptions

Transitivity. Indirect comparisons are valid only when the trials being combined enrolled comparable patients — similar disease stage, prior therapies, and other potential effect modifiers. If populations differ in ways that change how a treatment works, the indirect estimate may be biased.

Consistency. Wherever both direct and indirect evidence exist for the same comparison, they should agree. Disagreement is checked statistically (for example, node-splitting or design-by-treatment interaction models) and signals that the network may be combining studies it shouldn't.

How treatments get ranked

Once relative effects are estimated, every pair of treatments is summarized in a league table, and treatments are ranked by their probability of being among the best for each outcome. The most common ranking metrics are SUCRA (Surface Under the Cumulative RAnking curve, 0–100%) and its frequentist analog the P-score — higher values mean a treatment is more likely to be near the top.

Caveat — rankings should always be read alongside the underlying effect sizes and the certainty of the evidence (assessed with frameworks such as CINeMA or GRADE for NMA). A treatment can rank first while the supporting evidence is sparse or imprecise. Reporting follows the PRISMA-NMA extension.

Why NMA for TNBC?

Over the past decade, several novel agents — immune checkpoint inhibitors (PD-1, PD-L1), PARP inhibitors, and additional cytotoxics such as platinums — have been added to the chemotherapy backbone in early-stage TNBC. Most have been tested against chemotherapy in separate randomized trials, but very few have been compared head-to-head against each other. NMA is the right tool for this situation: it pulls every trial into a single network and estimates the comparative efficacy and safety of every regimen, even those that have never met in a trial.

Triple-Negative Breast Cancer (TNBC)

TNBC accounts for roughly 10–20% of all breast cancers and is an aggressive subtype with a high propensity for early relapse and poor prognosis. Because hormone receptors and HER2 are absent, endocrine therapy and HER2-directed agents are not options, leaving cytotoxic chemotherapy as the mainstay of treatment.

For early-stage disease, neoadjuvant chemotherapy is standard. Achieving a pathologic complete response (pCR) after neoadjuvant therapy is strongly associated with better long-term outcomes — but with conventional anthracycline–taxane regimens, only about 35–45% of patients reach pCR, leaving substantial room to improve.

Importance

Despite improvements in pathologic complete response (pCR) achieved by novel therapeutic combinations, the optimal neoadjuvant regimen for triple-negative breast cancer remains unclear — particularly when efficacy must be weighed against treatment-related hematologic toxicity.

Objective

To address this clinical gap, we conducted a systematic review and network meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials to evaluate the comparative efficacy and safety of neoadjuvant treatments in early-stage TNBC, with pCR as the primary outcome of interest.

Highlights

Phase 2-3 randomized controlled trials of neoadjuvant therapy in early and locally advanced TNBC were synthesized from Medline, Embase, and the Cochrane Library through August 2025, covering standard chemotherapy, PD-1 / PD-L1 inhibitors, and PARP inhibitors.
