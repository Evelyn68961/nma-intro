Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC), accounting for 10–20% of all breast cancers, is an aggressive subtype characterized by early relapse and poor prognosis. The absence of hormone receptor and HER2 expression precludes the use of endocrine or HER2-directed therapies, making cytotoxic chemotherapy the mainstay of treatment. In early-stage TNBC, neoadjuvant chemotherapy is standard, as pathologic complete response (pCR) is strongly associated with improved event-free and overall survival. Conventional anthracycline-, cyclophosphamide-, and taxane-based regimens achieve pCR rates of approximately 35–45%, but outcomes remain suboptimal. Recent clinical trials have evaluated intensified strategies such as the incorporation of platinum agents, immune checkpoint inhibitors, and other novel agents. However, the optimal neoadjuvant regimen is still uncertain, given the trade-offs between efficacy and toxicity and the lack of clear consensus across guidelines.
