Traditional pairwise meta-analyses are limited to summarizing direct evidence from randomized controlled trials (RCTs) that compare two interventions. However, in clinical areas where multiple competing treatments exist and head-to-head trials are scarce, these approaches are insufficient. Network meta-analysis (NMA), also known as mixed treatment comparison, extends conventional meta-analysis by simultaneously synthesizing both direct and indirect evidence across a network of interventions. This methodology allows for the estimation of relative treatment effects even when interventions have not been directly compared in trials and provides a framework for ranking therapies according to their efficacy and tolerability. As such, NMA is particularly valuable in conditions like early-stage triple-negative breast cancer, where numerous neoadjuvant regimens have been studied, but the optimal approach remains uncertain.
